1 Question

Since the late 1990’s, Global Positioning System (GPS) surveys have become a more accurate and reputable alternative to previous travel survey methods that collect activity-travel patterns. Despite GPS devices ability to record time and positional characteristics, they still require processing in order to convert the positional characteristics into trip purposes and activities.

The first step of this conversion process is cleaning the GPS data to produce trips and where they occurred. Currently, most researchers use rule-based algorithms to perform this task (Shen and Stopher 2014). These rules are time and speed thresholds, so they are subjective and difficult to apply to an entire sample population. One example would be that some people walk slower than others, so it is hard to determine a minimum speed that should classify movement. Another problem that rises with these rules is that every researcher must decide for themselves what they consider to be a trip. For example, one researcher may consider picking somebody up an activity and make their stop time threshold 2 minutes. Another researcher who does not consider this to be an activity would set their time threshold higher. Due to GPS data imputation being applied in different contexts, there is large variation in prediction accuracy, ranging anywhere from 70% and 85% (Feng and Timmermans 2016).

Besides rule-based algorithms, the next most common method is cluster-based. Instead of speed and time thresholds being used, the density of GPS points within a predefined radius determines an activity. These thresholds would not vary from person to person thus providing consistency and accuracy. In fact, one experiment using a DBSCAN cluster-based algorithms proved to be 92% precise (Luo et al. 2017).

Although this level of precision has been found when using clustering, three main gaps still remain: survey collection typically doesn’t exceed two weeks (Feng and Timmermans 2016), not all activities are accounted for in analysis, and this algorithm has not been published in R. Usually, researchers group all of the Other trip purposes into one category and analyze it as a whole (Elevelt et al. 2021).

Therefore, the question I am answering is “How does one write a cluster-based algorithm in R that accurately transforms 6+ months worth of GPS survey data into trips?”. The GPS data I used in my code is associated with mental healthy survey responses, so they are not publicly available. However, they will be generally described in the Methods section.
